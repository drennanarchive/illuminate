Visual Teaching & Communication

Pick what's happening in the room. The form and the diagram follow — drawn out so you can see exactly what to put on the whiteboard.

field guide ✦
1
Recognize the moment
Circling the same point"I keep explaining this and it's not landing"
Invisible relationships"These two things connect but I can't show how"
Before / after or cause & effect"Something changed — I need to show the delta"
Teachable pattern"They'll need to use this mental model again"
Scale or change over time"This grows in a way words can't capture"
Competing interpretations"We're using the same words but meaning different things"
Gut check: if you've said the same thing twice and it didn't land, that's your cue to draw it instead.

3
Draw it — pick what's happening
→ 2×2 quadrant axes first, then plot
Name the two things that were actually in tension as your axes. Plot alternatives in gray first. Star the chosen option last. Explain why others were ruled out in conversation — not on the board.
→ Box-and-arrow map draw from memory, star what matters today
Draw from memory — imperfect is more honest. Star the box that matters most today. Solid arrows = regular flow. Dashed = escalation or exception. Only go as deep as this conversation needs.
→ Spectrum + before/after let them place their dot first
The visual separates "the gap" from "the person" — which makes the conversation easier. Let them place their own dot first. It usually surfaces more self-awareness than you expected.
→ Cause-and-effect chain draw the flow before naming blame
Draw the flow first without naming anyone. Then ask "where did this enter?" together. Pointing to a box instead of a person changes the room. Circle the root cause. Box what would have caught it.
→ Contribution chain draw the downstream impact last
Draw the downstream impact last — that's the part they usually can't see. If you don't know what it is before the conversation, find out first. The chain makes "your work matters" visible, not just sayable.
→ Scope layer stack ask "which layer are we designing?" mid-meeting
When someone proposes a solution, ask "which layer is this?" and draw a line at that height. Stops the group from quietly designing full operations while thinking they're discussing the MVP.
✦
Always true — regardless of which template you use
▸
Draw the skeleton before the discussion starts. Axes, lanes, or layers drawn live while tracking conversation is the main failure mode. Even a rough grid changes the room.
▸
Separate capture → structure → decide. Get ideas up first (no judgment), cluster them, then mark what's actually decided — distinct from what was discussed.
▸
Imperfect and immediate beats polished and late. A rough sketch in the moment teaches more than a perfect slide deck next week. The act of drawing together is part of the teaching.
▸
Ask before you draw. "Can I sketch what I'm thinking?" gives the other person a moment to engage — they watch you build it rather than just receive it, which makes it stick.
▸
Close with a translation step. Say "so the board says X, Y, Z" out loud before the conversation ends. Catches misreadings while people are still there to correct them.
▸
Reserve a parking lot corner. A designated corner for tangents solves most derailed conversations. Label it out loud so people know their point was heard, just deferred.
