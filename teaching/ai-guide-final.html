Getting Started With AI

A friendly guide to using AI well

AI is software that understands what you ask and generates helpful responses.

Think: You talk to it. It listens. It gives you words back designed to help.

What it's good at

Research and understanding

Gathering background, explaining concepts, finding multiple angles on a topic.

Structuring ideas

Taking your rough thoughts and organizing them into a clear outline.

Brainstorming

Generating options when you're stuck or the well feels dry.

Editing and polish

Tightening prose, smoothing awkward sentences, improving flow.

What it's not

Infallible

AI can sound confident while being completely wrong. Always fact-check, especially on theology and citations.

An authority

It's research and brainstorming help. You make the final decisions based on Scripture and your integrity.

A time-saver for everything

Sometimes you're faster just writing it yourself. Use AI for tedious stuff, not everything.

A replacement for you

Your voice, your judgment, your theological understanding—those can't be outsourced.

AI doesn't always save time. But it's perfect for specific kinds of work. Knowing when to use it is key.

When AI actually helps

Research and background

Need context for a sermon? AI gathers background, finds perspectives, explains concepts. Then you write something only you could write.

Structuring big ideas

Have 10 rough ideas? AI helps organize them. You then fill in the actual content.

Brainstorming options

Stuck on planning an event? AI generates ideas. You pick what fits your people.

Editing and polish

You wrote something. It feels clunky. AI tightens it. You read the result, edit it, then send it.

When AI wastes your time

Quick, simple writing: A 2-line email? You're faster just writing it yourself.

Expecting AI to decide for you: You have three ideas. That's your job to pick.

Outsourcing your voice: Asking AI to write a post and posting it as-is. Your people will notice.

The real talk

AI saves time on tedium, not thinking. If the work is mostly thinking, AI won't help much.

You're accountable for everything. Whether you wrote it, AI wrote it, or you both did—your name is on it. Read it. Verify it. Own it.

How you ask AI to help determines what you get back. Clear questions get useful answers.

The 4-step workflow

1. Write your own first

Draft your sermon, post, or essay yourself. Your thinking is the foundation. AI fills gaps; it doesn't create from scratch.

2. Ask for a specific edit

Don't say "improve this." Say "Make this warmer" or "Add a question here." Tell AI exactly what to change.

3. Read and edit

Read what AI gives you. If it doesn't sound like you, change it. You're responsible for everything you share.

4. Ask for alternatives (if stuck)

If you're unsure, ask for 2–3 other ways to approach it. Get options. Pick the best one.

How to ask better

Vague
"Help me write about prayer"

Specific
"I'm writing a 10-min sermon on prayer for young adults. Focus on vulnerability."

Generic
"Improve this post"

Specific edit
"Make this conversational and authentic. Too formal for Instagram."

No context
"Suggest youth group ideas"

With context
"I lead ages 15–17. Skeptical but care about faith. Money is tight. What ideas work?"

Advanced tips

Tell AI your goal, not just your constraint

Better: "I want this welcoming to people hurt by the church"

Instead: "Make this not preachy"

Ask for different perspectives

Example: "How would a skeptic, a scholar, and someone struggling with faith understand this verse?"

Tell AI who you are

Example: "I'm a young pastor at a small church. Our people are working-class. I want this real, not preachy."

Context helps AI adapt instead of giving generic advice.

Example 1: Sermon Research ▼
You've read Romans 3:21-26. You have rough notes. But you need cultural background. Why was Paul's message radical? You want your congregation to understand.
You ask:

"I'm writing a sermon on Romans 3:21-26. My people are working-class and have struggled with shame. Why would first-century Jews have been shocked by Paul's teaching? Help me understand so I can explain it simply."

AI gives you:
  • Honor/shame culture (not just guilt)
  • Law as identity marker
  • How "grace, not works" would sound radical
  • Modern parallel: redefined by grace, not failures
You then:

The honor/shame angle resonates. That's your hook. You use AI's framework but write your own stories. You find someone in your congregation who struggled with shame. You use that. It's yours now.

Why this worked: You asked for background, not a sermon. AI gave angles. You stayed in charge.
Example 2: Social Media Post ▼
Your church did community cleanup. Neighborhood people came, not just church members. You want to post—genuine, not preachy, invite people back.
You ask:

"I'm posting about community cleanup. Neighborhood people came too. I want it real and welcoming. Invite people back without being pushy. Give me two versions—upbeat and reflective."

AI gives you:

Upbeat: "Loving your neighborhood isn't talk—it's action. Saturday we showed up. Church folks, neighbors, people we'd never met. Picked up trash. Ate pizza. That's community. Next month?"

Reflective: "Something shifts when you work alongside someone instead of just talking past them. Saturday proved it. Next month. Hope you will too."

You then:

Upbeat has the energy but feels stiff. You rewrite: "Saturday we cleaned up 5th Street. Church folks, neighbors, people who've never stepped foot in our building. Ate pizza together. That's community. Next month—come help." Now it's you.

Why this worked: You got options in different tones. You picked your direction. Then rewrote in your voice.
Example 3: Brainstorming ▼
Your church needs young adults ministry. You know some folks but you're blank on what to actually do together. Bible study feels boring to them.
You ask:

"Starting a young adults group. Most work long hours. Some married, some single. Skeptical of 'church stuff' but they care about faith. Give me 5 different monthly ideas—not Bible study. Mix faith, community, fun."

AI gives you:
  • Dinner and conversation (bring a real question)
  • Service project month (habitat, food bank)
  • Book/podcast discussions
  • Skill-share potluck
  • Panel nights (people doing interesting work)
You then:

"Dinner conversation" resonates. You start at your house. Invite 6 people. Say "Bring one question you're wrestling with." You ask AI: "Give me 10 open-ended faith questions for a dinner." You pick 3. Your event is now designed—but it's yours.

Why this worked: AI gave options when your mind was blank. You picked one and shaped it into something real.
