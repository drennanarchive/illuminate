A beginner’s guide

Using AI well.

AI can help you research, organize, brainstorm, and edit. The goal is not to let it think for you. The goal is to make your own thinking clearer and more useful.

01

What AI actually does

Think of AI as an eager assistant. It can be fast, useful, and surprisingly creative. It can also be confidently wrong.

AI works best when you give it a real task and stay involved. It can help you get started, sort through complexity, and improve a draft. It should not become the source of your judgment, beliefs, or decisions.

The golden rule: never stop thinking. You are still responsible for what you publish, teach, email, preach, or post.
RESEARCH

Find a useful starting point

Ask for key concepts, possible sources, competing viewpoints, or questions you should investigate next.

STRUCTURE

Organize messy information

Turn scattered notes into themes, an outline, a sequence, or a clearer explanation.

IDEAS

Generate possibilities

Create titles, examples, discussion questions, options, and angles you can react to.

EDIT

Improve your own draft

Make your writing clearer, shorter, warmer, more direct, or more appropriate for the audience.

The tools

You do not need every AI tool. Start with one or two, use them for real work, and learn their strengths over time.

Designed for Microsoft-centered work, including documents, spreadsheets, meetings, and workplace tasks.

Try it forDrafting in Word, analyzing Excel content, or recapping a Teams meeting.

A lightweight option for quick questions and casual help inside familiar social and messaging apps.

Try it forSimple idea generation when you do not need a long project.

Do I need to compare every tool before I start?

No. Choose the tool you can access easily and use it for a few real tasks. Skill comes from learning how to communicate with AI, evaluate its answer, and refine the work. Those habits transfer between tools.

03

When to use AI

Good AI use is not using it for everything. It is noticing where the tool genuinely improves your process.

AI is useful when…

  • You need a starting map for an unfamiliar topic.
  • Your notes are messy and need structure.
  • You want several options to react to.
  • Your draft needs a clearer tone or flow.
  • A task is repetitive, tedious, or difficult to organize.

Pause before using AI when…

  • You can finish the task more quickly yourself.
  • The information is sensitive or private.
  • You want AI to make the decision for you.
  • The work depends on personal care, spiritual discernment, or relationship context.
  • You are tempted to publish the first answer without reviewing it.
AI saves time on tedium. It does not remove the need for thought.
04

How to ask for better help

A useful prompt is simply a clear explanation of the task, audience, goal, and boundaries.

You do not need to memorize complicated prompt tricks. Talk to AI the way you would brief a capable assistant who does not yet know your situation.

I need to… Describe the task.
The audience is… Say who will read, hear, or use it.
The goal is… Explain what should happen next.
Please give me… Name the kind of output you want.
Keep it… Set the tone, length, and boundaries.
Too vague

“Write a church post.”

More useful

“Write a warm Facebook post inviting local families to our free six-week beginner martial arts program. Keep it friendly, clear, and under 120 words.”

Three follow-up questions that improve almost any answer

“What might I be overlooking?”

“Give me two very different alternatives.”

“Keep my meaning, but make this sound more like a real person.”

05

What good AI use looks like

The pattern is simple: you bring the purpose, AI helps with part of the process, and you review the result.

Researching a difficult topic

AI organizes, you evaluate
You ask“Map the major interpretations of this passage, explain the differences clearly, and give me questions to investigate. Do not treat any interpretation as settled fact.”
AI helpsIt creates a starting framework, identifies key terms, and surfaces areas of disagreement.
You finishYou check the claims against Scripture, course material, and reliable sources before forming your own conclusion.

Improving a church announcement

You bring the message, AI helps deliver it
You ask“Keep every fact the same, but make this warmer and easier to read. Do not make it sound like advertising.”
AI helpsIt improves the order, clarity, and tone of your original draft.
You finishYou restore any wording that sounds more like your church and verify the practical details.

Brainstorming an event

AI widens the options, you choose
You ask“Give me twelve low-cost discussion-night ideas for young adults at a small church. Avoid anything that feels forced or childish.”
AI helpsIt gives you a range of possible formats and themes.
You finishYou choose what fits your actual people, space, budget, and purpose.
A few mistakes AI makes surprisingly well

It may invent a quotation that sounds real, cite a source that does not exist, misunderstand a prompt, or answer a different question with complete confidence.

Fluency is not the same as truth. Important information still needs verification.

AI and ministry

AI can assist with organization, research questions, clarity, and idea generation. It should never replace prayer, biblical study, discernment, pastoral judgment, or care for actual people.

The takeaway

Use AI as a conversation, not an answer machine.

You become better at AI by asking, checking, editing, and trying again. A disappointing answer does not mean you failed. It usually means the conversation needs another turn.
