What AI actually does

Think of AI as an eager assistant: fast, useful, and sometimes confidently wrong.

The golden rule
Never stop thinking. You are still responsible for what you publish, teach, email, preach, or post.
R

Research

Find starting points, compare ideas, summarize long material, and help you see what needs verification.

S

Structuring

Turn scattered notes into outlines, categories, steps, or a clearer flow.

B

Brainstorming

Generate angles, examples, questions, titles, and possibilities you can react to.

E

Editing

Improve clarity, shorten text, adjust tone, and help your original ideas land better.

AI can invent facts, misunderstand your question, or sound certain when it is wrong. Important claims still need checking.
It can show perspectives and explain reasoning, but it does not get the final say,especially in theology, ethics, pastoral care, or personal decisions.
A two-sentence announcement may be faster to write yourself. AI helps most when the task is tedious, messy, unfamiliar, or hard to organize.
Your experience, judgment, relationships, faith, and voice are the valuable part. AI should support those,not flatten them.

Thoughtful work

Claude

Especially useful when you have a lot to read or a complicated idea to work through carefully.

Best for: Long documents, careful analysis, thoughtful writing, and organizing complex ideas.
Try it for: Comparing interpretations, reviewing a long draft, or building a structured paper outline.
Example request“Read these notes, identify the three main themes, and show me where my reasoning is unclear.”
Flexible all-rounder

ChatGPT

A strong choice when you want one conversational workspace for writing, planning, visuals, and practical help.

Best for: Brainstorming, drafting, editing, interactive guidance, research assistance, and creating useful artifacts.
Try it for: Refining a church post, planning an event, building a checklist, or iterating on an idea.
Example request“Here is my announcement. Keep my meaning, make it warmer, and give me one shorter alternative.”
Google-connected

Gemini

Most attractive when your day already happens inside Google’s ecosystem.

Best for: Quick information gathering and work connected to Google products.
Try it for: Working alongside Gmail, Docs, Drive, or other Google-based tasks.
Example request“Turn these meeting notes into a clean follow-up with decisions, owners, and next steps.”
Microsoft workflow

Copilot

Best understood as AI that is most useful when it sits beside Microsoft 365 work.

Best for: Documents, spreadsheets, email, meetings, and workplace tasks in Microsoft tools.
Try it for: Summarizing a Teams meeting, shaping a Word document, or analyzing an Excel table.
Example request“Summarize this meeting into decisions, unresolved questions, and actions by person.”
Quick and familiar

Meta AI

Useful for lightweight questions inside social or messaging apps you already use.

Best for: Fast, simple help without starting a larger project or workflow.
Try it for: Quick idea generation, casual questions, or simple wording help.
Example request“Give me five friendly ways to remind people that registration closes Friday.”
Simple starting advice Start with the tool you can access easily. Skill comes from practice,not collecting subscriptions.

When AI helps,and when it gets in the way

The best use of AI is not “everything.” It is knowing when the tool earns its place.

R

Research a starting point

Ask for concepts, keywords, perspectives, or questions to investigate,then verify important details.

O

Organize what you already have

Paste in rough notes and ask for themes, an outline, or a more useful order.

I

Generate possibilities

Ask for ten options so you can react, combine, reject, and sharpen your own thinking.

E

Improve a draft

Write first, then ask AI to make it clearer, shorter, warmer, or more appropriate for the audience.

If the message is obvious and takes thirty seconds, using AI may create more editing than it saves.
AI can help identify options and consequences. It should not carry the moral, spiritual, or relational responsibility for your choice.
If every post, sermon note, email, and announcement sounds machine-polished, people stop hearing you.
Reality check AI saves time on tedium,not on thinking.

How to ask for better help

Good prompting is mostly good communication: context, purpose, constraints, and a clear request.

1

Write first

Start with your own notes, opinion, outline, or rough draft whenever possible.

2

Ask specifically

Say what you need, who it is for, and what “good” should feel like.

3

Read and edit

Remove what sounds generic, check claims, and put your own language back in.

4

Ask for alternatives

Do not force one answer to work. Ask for another tone, angle, structure, or approach.

I need to… describe the task.
The audience is… say who will read or hear it.
The goal is… explain what should happen next.
Please give me… name the output you want.
Keep it… set the tone, length, or boundaries.
Too vague “Write a church post.”
Much better “Write a warm Facebook post inviting local families to our free six-week beginner martial arts program. Keep it friendly, clear, and under 120 words.”
Generic edit “Make this better.”
Specific edit “Keep my meaning and voice, but make this easier to understand and remove anything repetitive.”
No context “Give me discussion questions.”
With context “Give me eight discussion questions for young adults studying John 15. Start simple, then move toward personal application.”
“I want people to feel welcomed,” “I need leadership to make a decision,” or “I want this to sound like me” gives AI a useful target.
Try: “What might I be overlooking?” or “Show me how three different audiences could interpret this.”
Relevant context helps: your role, experience level, audience, beliefs, and how direct or polished you want the answer to be.

See the workflow in action

The point is not to copy the AI. The point is to use it as part of your own process.

You are preparing a Bible college paper and need a clearer starting map before reading primary sources.
You ask“Help me map the major interpretations of this passage. Explain the differences clearly, list questions I should investigate, and do not present any interpretation as settled fact.”
AI gives youA structured overview, key terms, competing viewpoints, and possible research questions.
You thenCheck the claims against Scripture, assigned readings, and reliable sources. Keep only what helps your own analysis.
Why this worked: You asked AI to organize the field,not to do your discernment or make the conclusion for you.
You already know what the announcement needs to say, but your first draft feels stiff.
You ask“Here is my draft. Keep every fact the same, but make it warmer and easier to read. Do not make it sound like advertising.”
AI gives youA cleaner version with smoother flow and a more welcoming tone.
You thenPut back any wording that sounds more like your church, remove anything over-polished, and verify the date and time.
Why this worked: You brought the message. AI helped with delivery.
You need ideas for a young adult discussion night, but you do not want a generic list.
You ask“Give me twelve discussion-night ideas for young adults. Make them low-cost, relational, and realistic for a small church. Avoid anything that feels forced or childish.”
AI gives youA range of ideas with different formats and energy levels.
You thenChoose two that fit your people, combine the best pieces, and adjust for your actual space and budget.
Why this worked: You used AI to widen the option set while keeping judgment in human hands.
AI invented a quotation that sounded biblical.
AI cited a source that did not exist.
AI answered a different question with total confidence.
The lesson: Fluency is not the same as truth. Verify anything important.
AI and ministry
AI can help organize your thoughts, summarize research, improve clarity, and generate possibilities. It should never replace prayer, biblical study, discernment, pastoral judgment, or care for actual people.
